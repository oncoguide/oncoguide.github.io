# OncoGuide -- Vision and Purpose

> **This document is the entry point to the project.**
> Every editorial, technical, or content decision must be consistent with what is written here.
> Read this before anything else.

---

## The patient is the primary end-user

Everything we build, write, or decide in OncoGuide is done for the patient.
Not for oncologists, not for search engines, not to demonstrate technology.
The reference question before any decision: **"Does this help the patient?"**

---

## Section 1: The Problem

An oncology patient lives in an era of abundant medical information -- but abundance does not mean relevance.

At diagnosis, everything hits at once: Google with contradictory results, stories from acquaintances, patient groups with diverse experiences, and an oncologist with 15 minutes to spare.

The problem is not a lack of information. The problem is that **the patient doesn't know what they don't know** -- they don't know what questions to ask, what to look for, what to avoid. And the consequences are concrete: the patient doesn't know what could interfere with their treatment, what could support it, how to recognize signs of progression, what options exist if the current treatment fails, what is emerging in the world that could help them.

Not because they didn't want to know. But because the information that directly concerns them -- filtered by molecular subtype, kept up to date, in their language -- does not exist anywhere in one place.

---

## Section 2: The Solution

OncoGuide is a curated oncology information resource -- not a generic medical education site, but a tool that filters, sorts, and delivers information relevant to your specific diagnosis, at the moment you need it.

**What "curated information" means:**
- Specific to the molecular subtype (not "lung cancer" -- "RET fusion-positive lung cancer")
- Updated monthly through AI curation from thousands of sources: clinical trials, drug approvals, oncology guidelines, trial data
- Verified and filtered -- not everything on the internet, but what is relevant and trustworthy
- In the patient's language, not the oncologist's

**For whom:**
Any oncology patient, regardless of disease stage -- newly diagnosed, in active treatment, at progression, or monitoring future options. Same resource, different information depending on your moment.

**Relationship with the oncologist:**
OncoGuide does not replace the oncologist -- it complements them. The patient comes to the consultation with the right questions. The oncologist has a more informed, more engaged patient. Both benefit. Even oncologists face information overload -- AI curation helps everyone stay up to date.

**Core promise:**
The information that can improve your prognosis exists. OncoGuide finds it for you.

## Section 3: Operating Principles

These principles answer the concrete question: **"Do we publish this or not?"**

### P1 -- Information serves survival, not curiosity

OncoGuide is not an oncology encyclopedia. We publish information that can improve the patient's prognosis -- the chance of survival with as few complications as possible. If a topic has no direct impact on the patient's decisions or health, we don't publish it.

### P2 -- Molecular subtype specific, never generic

Each guide is written for a precise diagnosis. "Lung cancer" is too vague -- patients with RET fusion, EGFR, KRAS have completely different treatments, prognoses, and side effects. A generic guide helps no one. We don't publish content that is not subtype-specific.

### P3 -- "What NOT to do" is just as important as "what to do"

A large part of effective treatment means avoiding mistakes -- drug interactions, contraindicated supplements, diagnostic delays, ignored warning signs. Each guide explicitly contains what must be avoided, not just what must be done.

### P4 -- The patient journey has 5 moments, all covered

A patient returns to OncoGuide at different moments. Our guides cover all stages:
1. **Diagnosis** -- what the diagnosis means, what tests are needed, what comes next
2. **Treatment optimization** -- what could interfere, what could support, how to maximize efficacy
3. **Monitoring** -- what to watch for, how to recognize problems early
4. **Progression** -- what progression means, what options exist, how to approach the next step
5. **Pipeline** -- what is being developed in the world, what clinical trials are relevant, what comes next

### P5 -- Updated monthly, otherwise it doesn't matter

Oncology evolves rapidly. A guide unchanged for 6 months may contain outdated information or miss new options. Our promise is monthly updates through the AI pipeline. If we cannot ensure updates, we don't publish the guide.

### P6 -- The patient's perspective, not the clinician's

We write as a patient who has been through it, not as a medical textbook. Medical terminology is explained immediately at first use. The tone is warm, direct, without condescension. We don't oversimplify -- we respect the patient's intelligence.

### P7 -- Partner to the oncologist, not a replacement

OncoGuide does not provide diagnosis, does not recommend individual treatments, does not replace medical consultation. We help the patient come prepared to the appointment -- with the right questions, the relevant information, the ability to understand what the doctor is telling them.

### P8 -- No commercial agenda, ever

No ads, no affiliate links, no product or institution endorsements. Conflict of interest destroys trust. Trust is OncoGuide's only asset.

### P9 -- Multilingual with real geographic context

Patients in Romania, Italy, France, Germany, Spain, and English-speaking countries have different access to medications and treatments. The guides reflect these differences -- we don't translate mechanically, we adapt to the patient's geographic reality.

- Not a generic oncology encyclopedia
